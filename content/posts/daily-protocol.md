# Introduction

I’ve spent quite some time refining my daily routine. Life can easily get messy if you don’t follow some sort of structure or protocol. At the same time, too much rigidity makes life unbearable. You need flexibility to adapt when circumstances change. No protocol is perfect. Still, I try to follow mine most of the time because it gives me clarity, efficiency, and peace of mind.

# Diet

I generally follow a balanced low FODMAP approach, which helps me avoid digestive discomfort and bloating while keeping meals nutrient-dense. I don't claim it is superior or helpful to everybody. Feel free to adjust my protocol and experiment on your own. I also limit saturated fats, salt, and sugars. I eat meat and eggs since they are very nutrient-dense, and if you opt for lean meat sources, the risk is minimal. I don't overconsume meat (100–150 g per portion is enough) and usually eat it 1–2 times per week. I also consume fish at least once per week.

## Breakfast

My go-to breakfast is a bowl of oatmeal with mix-ins that cover almost every nutrient bucket:

- Almond milk (200 ml) – light (~18 kcal/100 ml), doesn’t scorch or stick to cookware, and keeps cleanup simple
- Oats (40 g) – rich in fiber and beta-glucans
- Cacao powder (15 g) – high in flavonols, iron, fiber, and magnesium
- Hazelnuts (10 g) – vitamin E and monounsaturated fats; I get enough polyunsaturated fats from walnuts
- Walnuts (20 g) – good omega-3 source
- Brazil nut (1 piece) – nearly 100% of daily selenium
- Sunflower seeds (20 g) – vitamin E
- Chia seeds (15 g) – fiber-rich
- Blueberries (120 g, frozen) – anthocyanins for cognition and recovery
- Strawberries (2–3 pieces, frozen) – vitamin C to improve absorption of other nutrients
- Raspberries (30 g, frozen) – added taste variety
- Casein isolate (40 g, chocolate flavor) – creamy, slow-digestion protein for satiety

### Remarks

- Aim for ~30 g of mixed nuts daily; a meta-analysis associates 28 g/day with ~20% lower risk of cardiovascular disease and all-cause mortality ([Aune et al., 2016](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9776667/)). Nuts are easy to add into oatmeal. If you are allergic, swap for the ones that fit your needs.

- I use natural (undutched) cacao because it contains ~34.6 mg/g flavanols, versus ~13.8 mg/g (lightly dutched), ~7.8 mg/g (medium), and ~3.9 mg/g (heavy)—showing a dose-dependent decline with processing ([Miller et al., 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18710243/)). If you don’t want the extra effort, just use a dutched version from your local grocery store.

- Bananas pair well with chocolate, but they contain PPO enzymes that reduce flavanol absorption by up to 84% when co-consumed in smoothies ([Ottaviani et al., 2023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37615673/)). Better to eat them 1–2 h apart from high-flavonol foods. However, if you enjoy oatmeal with banana, don’t overcomplicate things—eat it with pleasure.

- Protein powder can be swapped for Skyr, Greek yogurt, or whey, but I prefer Casein Isolate for texture and satiety. Casein Isolate is not the same as Micellar Casein.

- Some people are concerned that sunflower seeds are high in omega-6, but systematic reviews and meta-analyses show they do **not** increase inflammatory markers ([Johnson & Fritsche, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22889633/); [Zhuang et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28752873/)).

- Taste often depends on protein flavor. Experiment until you find one you enjoy.

- I’ve tried making oatmeal with just water, but the taste isn’t great. I still add some water though, since 200 ml of milk alone isn’t enough liquid.

## Smoothie

A low-calorie, nutrient-dense afternoon smoothie:

- Kale (40 g) – vitamin K, lutein, zeaxanthin
- Chia seeds (15 g) – fiber
- Ginger (2 g) – digestion and flavor
- Pineapple (80 g, frozen) – flavor booster

### Remarks

- I freeze kale before blending it into my smoothies—this not only preserves nutrients like lutein and vitamin K but may also enhance ulforaphane production. While direct studies on kale are limited, research on broccoli sprouts shows that freezing at –20 °C can increase sulforaphane content by ~1.5–2.1×, likely by stimulating myrosinase activity ([Guo et al., 2015](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2015/ra/c5ra03403e)). Since kale contains the same precursor compounds (glucoraphanin) and enzyme (myrosinase), a similar boost is plausible. I chose kale over broccoli sprouts for convenience—this simple step helps me maximize the benefit without extra effort. Sulforaphane is particularly interesting because it activates the **Nrf2 pathway**, enhancing antioxidant defenses, lowering inflammation, and supporting cardiometabolic and brain health, although the effects are mostly in animal studies.

## Supper

Keep supper simple with sandwiches! A single sandwich has:

- Gluten-free bread slice – source of carbs and fiber
- Spread: avocado, hummus, olive tapenade – any source of fat that is healthier than butter
- One slice of low-fat cheese – protein and calcium, be careful with calories
- 3 slices of cucumber – not very nutritious, but adds crunch
- 5 cherry tomatoes
- Arugula – greens, high in nitrates, low in oxalates
- 1 egg – protein and choline

### Remarks

- Use whole-grain bread if tolerated. They have more fiber than plain bread, but who doesn’t love freshly baked ciabatta? You can make exceptions!
- Two eggs per day can cover a large part of the choline RDA, which is otherwise hard to achieve.
- You can choose any greens you want. I picked arugula because nitrates can improve energy production, lower blood pressure, and enhance sport performance, while keeping kidney stone risk low. Initially I wanted to add greens to smoothies, but I couldn’t get the taste right.
- Feel free to experiment. You don’t need to eat an egg every day. You can skip it completely or replace with tuna, tofu, or any protein you enjoy.

# Supplements

- **Omega-3** – Current evidence shows EPA and DHA may reduce cardiovascular events and heart-attack risk ([Yan et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36103100/)). I use an IFOS-certified supplement with ~650 mg EPA + 350 mg DHA ([Nutrasource IFOS overview](https://certifications.nutrasource.ca/about/how-certifications-work/ifos)).
- **Vitamin D (OTC, 2000 IU)** – adjust dosage based on lab results
- **Zinc Glycinate (5 mg)** – high bioavailability; avoid overdosing
- **B-Complex (100% RDA)** – an insurance policy; dosage stays within RDA, not megadoses.
- **Ashwagandha (KSM-66)** – systematic reviews show benefits for stress and anxiety, and doses ≥600 mg/day also improve sleep ([Akhgarjand et al., 2022](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36017529/); [Cheah et al., 2021](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0257843)).
- **Iron (OTC, 100 mg)** – if ferritin is low, take every other day for ~3 months. Alternate-day dosing optimizes absorption and minimizes hepcidin-mediated inhibition ([Stoffel et al., 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29032957/); [von Siebenthal et al., 2023](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/eclinm/article/PIIS2589-5370%2823%2900463-7/fulltext)).

## Remarks

- There is no need to pair Vitamin D with Vitamin K2. RCT studies didn't confirm its benefits for preventing calcifications. It also doesn't hurt to take with K2, however you won't find Vitamin D3+K2 as an OTC drug. Having said that I prefer to use OTC drug as I know what is inside. Supplements are highly unregulated and can contain different dosages than the one written on the packaging.
- Adjust dosage of Vitamin D based on the lab results. Aim for the minimal effective dosage. Keep into equation the season e.g. you might need a different dosage during summer vs. winter.

## Running

3x/week, balancing intensity and endurance:

**Training A (Zone 2 + Speedwork):**

- 45min Zone 2 + 20 × 15s fast / 15s jog + 5min cooldown

  or

- 45min Zone 2 + 10 × 20s fast / 40s jog + 5min cooldown

**Training B1 (VO2Max):**

- 15min (Zone 2) + 6 × 3min (Zone 5) run / 3min (Zone 2) jog + 5min cooldown

  or

- 15min (Zone 2) + 4 × 4min (Zone 5) run / 3min (Zone 2) jog + min cooldown

**Training B2 (Threshold):**

- 15min Zone 2 + 3 × 10min (Zone 4) run / 2min (Zone 2) jog + min cooldown

  or

- 15min Zone 2 + 4 × 8min (Zone 4) run / 2min (Zone 2) jog + min cooldown

**Training C (Long Run):**

- 75–90min Zone 2

Zones should be lab-tested. Estimation tools have never matched well for me. Each week you can switch between training B1 and B2. Keep in mind that VO2Max training as a beginner will be brutal, you might be able only to maintain a single 4min interval. If that's the thing, no worries, it's okay. Make your workout shorter and try to improve next week.

# Sleep

Bedtime between 22:00–23:00—too late and you miss deep sleep.

- Prioritize consistency
- Summer wool sheet + bamboo cover (soft and breathable)
- Keep room ~22 °C; lower radiator in winter, use AC in summer
- Maintain 40–60% humidity to prevent mold
- Earplugs + silk mask help me fall asleep
- Ventilation: open a window if it's quiet; otherwise crack a window in another room with the door left open

## Sleep-Related Supplements

I don’t use:

- Magnesium (diet sufficient; weak sleep evidence)
- Tart cherry juice (optional for recovery)
- Apigenin (not enough human data)
- Melatonin (effective, but gives me rebound insomnia)
- OTC antihistamines (grogginess)

I do use:

- Glycine (3 g before bed) – improves subjective sleep quality and reduces fatigue ([Yamadera et al., 2007](https://consensus.app/questions/does-glycine-help-sleep/))
- Kiwi (see above)
- A warm shower before bed – paradoxically lowers core body temperature and promotes sleep
